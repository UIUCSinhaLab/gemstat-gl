Quantitative modeling of a gene's expression from its intergenic sequence

Md. Abul Hassan Samee1* and Saurabh Sinha1,2

1Department of Computer Science and 2Institute for Genomic Biology, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, Urbana, Illinois, USA

*To whom correspondence should be addressed, samee1@illinois.edu

 

Abstract

Modeling a gene's expression from its intergenic locus and trans-regulatory context is a fundamental goal in computational biology. Such models facilitate our studies of gene regulation at a mechanistic level and can reveal how perturbations in the regulatory machinery of a gene impact its spatio-temporal expression. Models of enhancer function, despite their remarkable success in the past decade, fall short of achieving the broader goal of modeling a gene's expression from its entire locus. In particular, the distributed nature of cis-regulatory information and the less understood mechanisms that integrate such information place gene locus modeling in a different realm than that of modeling an individual enhancer. Here we report the first quantitative model of a gene's expression pattern as a function of the sequence of its entire locus. As per this model, the expression readout of the locus is two-tiered: 1) the combinatorial effect of transcription factor binding sites within each enhancer is predicted by a thermodynamics-based model, and 2) independent contributions from multiple enhancers are linearly combined to produce the gene's expression pattern. The model does not require any prior knowledge about locations of the enhancers contributing toward gene expression. We demonstrate that the model captures the complex multi-domain expression patterns of genes even-skipped, hairy, runt, and giant along the anterior-posterior axis of the early Drosophila embryo. Our findings suggest the presence of sequence segments in the locus that would contribute ectopic expression patterns and thus have to be "shut down" by the model. Such segments were found to be located in inaccessible regions of the locus. We applied our model to identify the transcription factors responsible for the formation of stripe boundaries of the studied genes. The resulting network of regulatory interactions exhibits a high level of agreement with known regulatory influences on the target genes. Finally, we used the model to analyze interactions between multiple enhancers in a gene locus. We tested the common assumption of independent enhancer activities and interrogated how information is integrated from multiple enhancers to produce a gene expression pattern. We found that enhancers of the four genes studied in this work act mostly as autonomous entities to drive expression of their targets.
